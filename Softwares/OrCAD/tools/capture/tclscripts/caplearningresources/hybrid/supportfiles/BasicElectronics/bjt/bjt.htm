Bipolar Junction Transistors

This section discusses Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJT) and explains how to simulate various circuits based on BJT in OrCAD® Capture-PSpice®. 

The transistor or BJT is a three-layer semiconductor device with two outer layers of same charge layers and a thin inner layer of a different charge. The layers are called emitter, base, and collector. The materials with excess electrons (negative charge) are called N type and materials with excess holes (positive charge) are called P type. If the base is of N type and the emitter and collector of P type, the BJT is a PNP type. If the base is of P type and the emitter and collector of N type, the BJT is of NPN type.

Figure 1. BJTs symbols

The emitter layer is heavily doped, the base layer is only lightly doped, and the collector layer is also lightly but more than base. The outer layers, collector and emitter, have much greater width than the sandwiched p- or n- type base layer. 

Basic Working of a Transistor

The emitter-base junction of a transistor is forward biased, while the base-collector junction is reverse biased. In an NPN transistor due to forward biasing of the emitter-base junction, the electrons that are the majority carriers diffuse from the emitter to the base region. As the  base region is very lightly doped and is small in width, the diffused electrons act as minority charge carriers; most of them cross the reverse biased junction of base-collector region resulting in heavy collector current. Only a few of the electrons diffused in the base region contribute to the base current.

To observe BJT characteristic curve in common-base configuration use the circuit shown in figure (3) below, here the emitter-base junction is forward biased and base-collector junction is reverse biased.

Figure 3. BJT in common base configuration

To observe input characteristic curve, which is between VEB and IE, voltage V2 has to be varied. The following simulation settings are made:

  • Select DC Sweep analysis type.
  • Enter Voltage source name as V2.
  • With sweep type set to Linear, enter the Start value as 0V, the End value as 1V, and  the Increment as 0.01V

Note that forward drop across emitter base junction is approximately 0.6V which is equivalent to the forward biased p-n junction’s forward voltage drop. After the forward voltage drop, the emitter current reaches to the level of a few milliamperes.

Emitter follower

In the emitter-follower configuration, the output is taken from the emitter terminal of a transistor with a common collector terminal. To observe functioning of this configuration, use the circuit shown in figure (11) below:

Figure 11. BJT in common collector configuration acting as an amplifier

Figure 12. Simulation output

Figure 13. Cursor window

In the emitter-follower configuration, as the voltage at the base begins to rise, the emitter-base depletion region starts to narrow until the base-emitter junction becomes forward-biased. Once forward-biased, the base-emitter (p-n) junction can be thought of as a conducting diode where the voltage at the base (anode) is greater than that at the emitter (cathode) by 0.6 V. In other words, the emitter follows the base.

BJT as Switch

To observe transistor application as a switch following circuit with common emitter configuration as shown in figure (14) can be used:

Figure 14. BJT in Common emitter configuration to act as a switch

Figure 15. Simulation output

As observed from the waveform shown in figure (15), from 0 to 100ms the BJT is in the saturation region because both base-emitter and base-collector junctions are forward-biased. In this condition, there is ideally a short between the collector and the emitter, and the collector current (IC) has a finite value equal to 97.7mA. The circuit behaves as a closed switch with ON condition

In the region 100ms to 500ms the BJT is in the cutoff region because the base-emitter junction has not been forward-biased. Ideally no current flows and the BJT behaves similar to an open switch, with OFF condition.

Kirchoff’s Voltage Law

In a closed loop network, the voltage drop within the same loop is equal to the sum of all the voltage drops. Since the start and end points are identical, there is no potential difference between them. Therefore, voltage  is equal to zero.

 (2)

Kirchoff’s Current Law

At any node or junction in an electrical circuit, the sum of the charge flowing into that node is equal to the sum of the charge flowing out of that node.

This law is  based on a simple fact that charge can neither be created nor destroyed.

Thevenin's Theorem

Any two-terminal linear network composed of voltage sources, current sources, and resistors, can be replaced by an equivalent two-terminal network consisting of an independent voltage source, Vthevenin (Vth) in series with a single resistor, Rthevenin (Rth). The value of Vth is the open circuit voltage at the terminals, and the value of Rth is Vth divided by the current with the voltage being short circuited.

Norton's Theorem

A linear network consisting of several independent or dependent energy sources and resistances is electrically equivalent to a single constant current source in parallel with a single resistor. The value of resistance is the same as that in the Thevenin Equivalent and the charge can be determined by dividing the open circuit voltage by resistance.

Superposition Theorem

The total charge in any part of a linear network consisting of several independent or dependent energy sources and resistances is electrically equivalent to the sum of the charge produced by each source separately. The resulting circuit is made by using one source at a time with all the other current sources being open circuited and the voltage sources being short circuited.

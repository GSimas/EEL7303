Semiconductor Diodes

This section discusses diode and explains how to simulate various circuits based on diodes in OrCAD® Capture-PSpice®.

Semiconductors are materials with a small gap between the conduction and valence bands unlike conductors which have overlapping bands and insulators where the gap is very large. Also unlike metals, the resistance of a semiconductor decreases with the increase in temperature. Semiconductors can be doped by adding impurities to change their behavior and make them N or P type. The semiconductors doped with donor impurities have excess electrons (negative charge) and are termed N type. Similarly, semiconductors doped with acceptor impurities have excess holes (positive charge) and are termed P type. 

If donor impurities are introduced into one side and acceptors into the other side of a single semiconductor crystal, a p-n junction is formed. The electrical characteristic of such a junction is that it permits easy flow of charge in one direction while restraining the flow in the other. A semiconductor diode is a p-n junction with two terminals that allow easy flow of current in direction.

Biasing Conditions

Diode in No Bias Condition: The p-type region of a diode has excess holes and the n-type region has excess electrons. If a p-n junction is not biased or is an open circuit, there exists a density gradient across the junction. In this condition, holes initially diffuse to the right (n side) and electrons to the left (p side). Due to this movement, a few positive charges or acceptors are left in the n-side and a few negative charges or donors are left in the p-side. This forms a region called the depletion region that creates a potential barrier. This depletion region prevents the further exchange of majority charge carriers across the region.

Diode in Forward Biased Condition: A p-n junction diode is forward biased by connecting a voltage source in such a way that the p-side is connected to the positive terminal and the n-side to the negative terminal. This forces the electrons in n-type region and holes in p-type region to recombine with ions near the boundary, reducing the width of the depletion region. This results in heavy majority charge flow across the junction. There is a minimum cut-off voltage below which no current flows across the junction and after that voltage and current rise.

Diode in Reverse Biased Condition:-A p-n junction diode is reverse biased by connecting a voltage source in such a way that the p-side is connected to the negative terminal and the n-side to the positive terminal. In the reverse biased condition, the number of acceptors or positive ions in the depletion region of the n-type material increase due to the large number of free electrons drawn to the positive ions in the depletion region of the n-type material. Similarly, the number of negative ions or donors increases in the p-type material. This widens the depletion region creating a great barrier for the majority carriers and effectively reducing the majority carrier flow to zero. But the number of minority carriers entering the depletion region will not change, resulting in minority carrier flow of the same magnitude as with no applied voltage.

Clipper Circuits

When a diode is used in a circuit to clip away a portion of an input signal without distorting the remaining part of the applied waveform, it is a known as a clipper. Clipping of input signal is achieved by using the property of a diode to conduct only when forward biased. There are two types of clipper circuits, series and parallel.

In series clipper circuits, output is taken across the load. For example, in the circuit in figure (5), output is taken across the load R1.

Clamper Circuits

A clamper circuit shifts a waveform to a different DC level without changing the appearance of the applied signal. The circuit consists of a diode, a resistor, and a capacitor as shown in figure (13). The capacitor is connected directly from input to output with a resistive element in parallel with the output signal. The diode is also in parallel with the output signal but may or may not have a series DC supply as an added element. Additional shifts can be easily obtained by introducing a DC supply to the basic structure.

Figure 13. Clamper circuit with diode forward biased

Figure 14. Resultant waveform


After simulating the circuit in figure (13) it can be observed that:

  • When the input pulse is positive, the capacitor is quickly charged to 8V. But because the diode is forward biased, the output voltage observed is equal to the forward voltage drop of diode approximately 0.7V
  • When the input pulse is negative, the output voltage obtained is approximately -8V-8V+0.7V = -15.3V (where the additional -8V is due to voltage across the capacitor and 0.7V is the initial voltage)

Zener Diode
Figure 17. Typical symbol of zener diode

The reverse-voltage characteristic of a semiconductor diode designed with adequate power-dissipation capabilities to operate in the breakdown region is known as a zener diode. When the reverse voltage is greater than the knee or zener voltage, the diode allows very high reverse current through it while maintaining the zener voltage across it.

Voltage versus current characteristic can be viewed by simulating the circuit shown below in figure (18). Here simulation type is DC sweep in which voltage source V1 is swept from 0 to 5V with an increment of 0.01.

Zener regulator

Zener diodes regulate the load voltage against variations in load current and supply voltage due to their capability to produce only small changes in diode voltage for large changes in diode current when operating in breakdown region. As load current or supply voltage changes the diode current will accommodate itself to these changes to maintain a nearly constant load voltage. The diode will continue to regulate until the diode current falls in the neighborhood of the knee voltage of the diode volt-ampere characteristic.
